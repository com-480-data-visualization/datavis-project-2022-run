Kobe Bryant 8/24



Young, fresh, excited and new. But not naive. And certainly not intimidated. At the innocent age of 18, and just months removed from his high school graduation line, Kobe Bryant was in the NBA, and neither would ever be the same. His bounce and energy and amazing basketball maturity was evident from the jump. The special athletes look like they belong the moment they reach the highest of levels. And Kobe belonged, as the Warriors discovered one night in his rookie season when he showed the variety of skills that would last 20 seasons.


After a rookie season in which he dropped numerous hints of the promise that the Lakers and many others projected for him, Kobe was officially on a star watch. The Lakers brought him along slowly, careful to make sure he didn’t step into many potholes along the journey, and the process was necessary because of his youth. That said, it was evident, from his teammates and then-coach Del Harris, that Kobe was moving in the right direction, at the right speed. So much was at stake: His career, and the team’s future.


“That’s what makes the mark of a star, how he makes his players better.” Those words were from Lakers coach Phil Jackson, issuing a challenge to Kobe to add a much-needed element to his game and his mindset. And so it all came together for Kobe during his MVP season, when he said “Ain’t going to the shootout with butter knives no more. I got guns now -- now we gon’ see what’s up.” That was a response not just to the Lakers gifting him Pau Gasol and Lamar Odom, but also Kobe doing what he could to elevate his teammates’ confidence and performances. It made for a magical ride. This is Kobe at his all-around best. This is Kobe in MVP form.

Explosive Three-Pointer



The ABA, which was playing against the NBA at the time, was the first to adopt the 3-pointer at the start of the 1967-68 season, but at that time the 3-pointer was just a gimmick to attract fans, and its tactical importance was not recognized until the 21st century.


In the 1975-76 season before the merger with the NBA, ABA three-pointer Bill Keller ranked first in the league with 349 attempts and 123 three-pointers, and third in 35.2 percent. The current Celtic president, Danny Ainge, made 148 three-pointers in the same season, making him the first player in NBA history to break the 100-ball mark in a single season.




"Defense" is one of the biggest changes in the league over the past 20 years. This change is the result of the NBA's continuous narrowing of defensive space and rule revisions in favor of offense over the past 20 years. Since the Pistons in the late 1980s used fierce defense as the basis for winning consecutive championships, "defense" has become the basic condition for the team to win in the 1990s, but the fans who yawned on the sidelines and in front of the TV did not like it.


The increasingly slow pace has forced the NBA authorities to look for ways to shift the game to a fast-paced offense. In addition to making an issue of the distance from the three-point line, the NBA has also begun to make major changes to the defensive rules, which mainly focus on two aspects: Hand Check and Zone Defense. This rule change has also become the most obvious watershed in the style of play. Not only has the average score per game increased from 93.4 points to 97.2 points, but the "effective field goal percentage (eFG%)" used to evaluate the power of three-pointers also increased from 47.1% to 48.2%, and then increased year by year to stabilize at around 50% in recent years.




The Warriors, who dominated the 2015 season, ranked fourth in the league with 2,217 3-point attempts but made 883 shots on a staggering 39.8 percent. The evolution of the three-point shot has not only changed the way points are scored, but it has also had an even bigger impact on the game of basketball. It's not just how many more long shots than in the past, it's completely overturning the thinking of position allocation on the basketball court.
